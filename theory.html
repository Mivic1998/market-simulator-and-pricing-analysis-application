Market Simulator Theory Guide

Overview

This simulator demonstrates how supply and demand interact in a market economy. It allows users to explore equilibrium, taxation, revenue maximisation, consumer surplus, producer surplus, and deadweight loss.

The application includes multiple demand curve types to represent different economic assumptions and consumer behaviours.

Demand Curves

Linear Demand

The linear demand curve follows the equation:

P = a - bQ

  • a represents the vertical intercept or maximum willingness to pay.
  • b measures price sensitivity.

Linear demand is commonly used because it is simple and allows clear visualisation of equilibrium, taxation, and elasticity effects.

Nonlinear Demand

Nonlinear demand introduces curvature into the demand relationship. This allows the simulator to model markets where consumer responsiveness changes at different price levels.

Unlike linear demand, nonlinear demand can represent situations where consumers become much more sensitive to price changes as prices rise.

Income-Based Demand

The income-based demand curve is derived from a Cobb–Douglas utility function.

The underlying utility function is:

U(x,y)=x^k y^(1-k)

where:

  • x represents consumption of the good in the simulator.
  • y represents all other goods.
  • k represents the consumer's preference share for the good.

When consumers maximise this utility subject to their budget constraint, the resulting demand function becomes:

x = (kI)/P

where:

  • I is consumer income.
  • P is price.

This means demand increases with income and decreases with price. The model is useful because it connects demand directly to microeconomic utility theory.

Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium occurs where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.

At equilibrium:

  • there is no excess demand,
  • there is no excess supply,
  • the market clears efficiently.

The simulator calculates equilibrium automatically by finding the intersection between the supply and demand curves.

Consumer and Producer Surplus

Consumer Surplus

Consumer surplus measures the difference between what consumers are willing to pay and what they actually pay.

Graphically, it is the area below the demand curve and above the market price.

Producer Surplus

Producer surplus measures the difference between the market price and the minimum price producers are willing to accept.

Graphically, it is the area above the supply curve and below the market price.

Taxation and Deadweight Loss

A per-unit tax shifts the effective supply curve upward. Consumers pay a higher price while producers receive a lower price.

The tax creates:

  • government revenue,
  • a reduction in quantity traded,
  • deadweight loss.

Deadweight loss represents the lost gains from trade caused by market distortion. It appears as the triangular welfare loss area between supply and demand.

Total Revenue

Total revenue is calculated as:

TR = P × Q

The simulator also identifies the revenue-maximising point on the demand curve. For linear demand, this occurs where marginal revenue equals zero.

Elasticity

Elasticity measures how responsive quantity demanded is to changes in price.

  • Elastic demand: quantity changes strongly when price changes.
  • Inelastic demand: quantity changes only slightly when price changes.

Elasticity affects tax incidence, revenue generation, and welfare loss.
